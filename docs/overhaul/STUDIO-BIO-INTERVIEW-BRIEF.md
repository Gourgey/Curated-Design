# Studio bio interview brief (P4.6)

## Why this exists

The Studio page (`src/content/pages/about.json`, rendered by `src/about.njk`) currently covers philosophy, process, and location/scope — but no named person, no biography, and no portrait or environmental photo. Build-plan ticket P4.6 requires "introduce the person or studio behind the work with approved imagery and facts," and that can't be drafted or invented — it has to come from a real interview. This brief is for whoever conducts that interview (which may just be Josh interviewing himself with a notes doc — that's fine too).

**Ground rule carried over from the rest of this build plan: no invented or embellished claims.** Every other content ticket in this project (see P3.8's metadata work, for example) has stuck to recombining or citing facts the site owner actually confirmed — never guessing at something that sounds plausible. Skip a question rather than let the interviewer or the writer fill in a plausible-sounding answer.

## How to use this

Work through Section A and B as a conversation, not a form — let answers run long, then edit down together afterward. Section C is a separate, practical checklist for arranging the photography once you know what's being shot. Section D is optional deeper material if the conversation has room for it.

Anything marked **(optional)** can be skipped without blocking the page — leaving it out just means that part of the page stays as it is now (philosophy/process only, no personal framing added).

---

## Section A — Who is behind Curated Design

1. Name, and the title/role to use publicly (Founder? Principal Designer? Director?).
2. In a sentence or two, what's your background before or alongside Curated Design — training, prior firms, other relevant experience?
3. How long has the studio been operating, and is that something you want stated on the page (exact year, or a vaguer "since 20XX")?
4. Is Curated Design a solo practice, or is there a team? If a team, does anyone else need a name/credit on this page, or does the page stay focused on one person as the public face?
5. What got you into interior design specifically — the real, non-generic version of this answer, not the marketing-copy version. (This is the thing readers actually remember; vague "passion for design" language is the thing to avoid.)
6. Any credentials, qualifications, professional memberships, or press/recognition worth naming? Only include what you're comfortable being fact-checked on.
7. Is there a personal thread connecting you to the studio's actual focus areas (residential, hospitality, spatial planning, furniture/lighting — see the existing Focus Areas on the page) — e.g. a project type you're known for, or a client type you work best with?

## Section B — How you actually work (optional, but strengthens credibility)

8. The page already describes a three-step process (Brief & concept → Design development → Delivery & styling) in fairly abstract terms. Is there a real, specific example (without breaching client confidentiality) that shows what that looks like in practice?
9. What do you want a prospective client to know about working *with you specifically*, beyond what the process steps already say?
10. Anything from your background that explains the studio's stated point of view — "layouts that genuinely work, robust materials, a palette that can evolve," clean lines and warm proportions, joinery/hardware/finish-level detail (all already on the page)?

## Section C — Photography checklist

The page needs at least:

- **One portrait.** Should read as calm, tactile, quietly confident — matching the studio's existing visual language (the same tone as the interior photography already on the site: warm, uncluttered, natural light, not corporate-headshot stiff). Landscape or portrait orientation both work; confirm during layout which the template needs.
- **One environmental/in-context shot (optional but recommended).** You at work — on site, at a material selection, sketching, mid-conversation with a client or contractor. This is what separates "stock-photo trustworthy" from "actually real."

Practical requirements for whoever shoots or selects the images:

- High resolution, and rights-cleared for web use on this site specifically (confirm ownership/licensing before it goes in front of the CMS — see the existing rights-and-credits diligence already established for project photography in `docs/overhaul/CONTENT-INVENTORY.md`).
- No baked-in text, watermark, or studio logo overlay in the photo itself — the page renders the name/title as real HTML, not as part of the image (this project has an active, ongoing effort — P3.7 — to remove exactly this kind of baked-in text from other images on the site; a new photo with the same problem would just create a fourth instance of it).
- A short, accurate alt-text description of each image for accessibility (e.g. "Studio founder reviewing material samples on-site" — descriptive, not a caption dressed up as alt text).

## Section D — Optional deeper material

If there's time and appetite:

- A short, real client or project anecdote that illustrates the studio's approach (with permission if it identifies the client/project).
- What the studio explicitly *doesn't* do, or won't compromise on — specificity like this tends to build more trust than a list of things it does do.
- Anything about London/the studio's working locations (already covered factually elsewhere on the page) that has a personal angle worth adding here rather than there.

## After the interview

Hand the raw notes back for drafting — the actual page copy should be written from what was actually said, not paraphrased into generic studio-bio language. If any answer above was skipped, that's a signal to leave the corresponding page section out entirely rather than pad it.
